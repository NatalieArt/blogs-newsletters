Why you no longer need to outsource your architectural renders.

Start from one concept sketch, turn it into a polished architectural still, expand it into multiple camera angles, and finish with a presentation-ready reveal video inside Sogni.

What you need

  • One sketch or concept image
  • Access to Sogni Create for the first pass
  • 360 by Sogni for alternate angles and still sets
  • An initial direction for materials, landscaping, lighting, and mood
From concept to presentation

Take complete control of your architectural presentations.

Instead of waiting through a slow visualization back-and-forth, this workflow keeps the entire sequence creator-controlled. You start with one unfinished concept, shape the first render in Sogni Create, expand the still set in 360 by Sogni, and only then decide whether the job needs motion.

The article that lived on the old domain framed the process in three parts, and that structure still holds up: one source image, one editing pass, and one optional motion layer at the end.

Input

One sketch or concept image is enough to begin the workflow.

Process

Edit inside Sogni Create, then broaden the scene in 360 by Sogni.

Outcome

A presentation-ready still set, with optional transitions and reveal video.

Why it matters

It does not replace architecture. It removes visualization drag.

“This workflow does not replace technical architecture. It removes the slow, expensive visualization back-and-forth and gives you a creator-controlled process you can run yourself.”

Step 1

Build the first polished still in Sogni Create.

The original article starts in the browser for a reason: speed. You can jump directly into the image workflow without needing a separate production round. The recommended opening model is Qwen Image Edit, with Flux 2 kept in reserve when the job needs multiple references at once.

Upload the referenceBring in the original sketch, unfinished concept, or mood frame that should anchor the project.
Write the prompt around materials and moodCall out finishes, light, landscaping, glazing, and the exact atmosphere you want the first render to establish.
Enhance the best stillOnce one render locks the idea, refine that frame before you expand the scene into more viewpoints.

The key is not to chase every variation at once. The first still becomes the source of truth for the rest of the still set.

Step 2

Open 360 by Sogni and turn one still into a wider presentation set.

After the first strong still, the old walkthrough moves directly into 360 by Sogni. That is where the concept stops being a single shot and starts becoming a usable presentation package.

Review the still setUse the first polished frame as the stable source and expand into additional angles with more confidence and less drift.
Preserve the structureKeep the identity of the design while widening the camera, shifting elevation, or changing the emphasis of the composition.
Choose what is worth presentingYou do not need every variation. You need the few angles that tell the story of the design best.

This is the part that makes the workflow feel viable for real presentation work. One image turns into a coherent still set instead of a pile of unrelated renders.

Step 3

Add motion only after the stills are solved.

The original article treats motion as an optional finishing layer, not the first move. That is the right order. Once the still set is working, you can generate transitions, render the video, and build a reveal that feels tied to the architecture instead of floating away from it.

Generate transitions Render the video Create the reveal

That sequence is especially strong for client presentations: establish the design in still frames, then use motion to elevate delivery rather than rescue weak visuals.

Next move

Use this flow whenever you need speed without giving away control.

The strongest takeaway from this article is not a single prompt or model. It is the order of operations: sketch first, hero still second, still set third, motion last. If you keep that sequence, the work stays coherent and the final presentation feels intentional.
