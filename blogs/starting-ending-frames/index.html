Starting & Ending Frames.

Control where a clip begins, where it lands, and how it connects to the next one. Starting and Ending Frames are one of the cleanest ways to hold continuity and extend motion across generations.

Pro move

Reuse the last frame of one clip as the starting frame of the next clip. That one habit makes longer sequences feel much more coherent.

Continuity first

Starting and Ending Frames are one of the best tools for continuity.

The old article makes the case clearly: video models are great at motion, but they drift. A strong Starting Frame anchors how the clip opens, and an Ending Frame nudges how the shot lands. That gives you a cleaner destination, better loops, and a much easier way to extend the sequence.

Why it matters

Identity, composition, and motion stay more consistent from clip to clip.

How it works

Start Frame anchors the opening. End Frame guides where the motion resolves.

What scales

Longer chained sequences that stitch better in Mixer and refine better in Timeline.

The core logic

Once the first frame pair works, the rest of the sequence becomes much easier to chain.

“Set the Starting Frame and Ending Frame based on how you want the video to begin and end. Then write what should happen between those two frames.”

Step 1

Open Image to Video in Sogni Create and pick the workflow that matches the job.

The source walkthrough starts in Sogni Create, using the LTX video model in Image to Video mode as the fastest way to begin once you already know the first and last frame you want. It also calls out Wan Image to Video as an option when you are willing to trade more time for a different generation profile.

Fast first passLTX is the quickest place to prove the frame pair and the motion idea.
Beginner-friendly setupThe workflow is intuitive enough to get a strong first pass before moving deeper into Studio.
Flexible follow-upOnce one clip works, you can keep extending or move the result into more advanced editing.
Step 2

Set the first frame pair you want to turn into a clean transition.

The article frames this as the central creative decision. You pick the opening state, the destination state, and then describe what should happen between them: transformation, pacing, camera feel, or motion style.

The End Frame stays optional, but it is especially useful when you want to reduce drift, encourage cleaner loops, or make the final landing feel less random.

Anchor the opening Guide the landing Write the in-between motion
Step 3

Build the sequence one clip at a time, then stitch automatically in Mixer.

After the first pair works, the same pattern scales. Use one clip’s End Frame as the next clip’s Start Frame, regenerate until the motion is right, and keep extending the chain. That is where the workflow becomes production-friendly rather than novelty-friendly.

Clip by clipKeep each short generation focused and intentional instead of forcing one long shot to do everything.
Stitch in MixerCombine the clips automatically once the frame chain is working.
Fine-tune in TimelineRefine pacing and sequencing after the clip chain already feels structurally sound.
Next move

Use frame pairs whenever continuity matters more than surprise.

This workflow is strong because it keeps the model expressive without giving away the structure of the shot. Use it for tutorials, polished product motion, seamless transitions, and any sequence that has to feel intentional from clip to clip.
